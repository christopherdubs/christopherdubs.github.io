CHARMED

v 1.0

CHARMED is a standard of quality: Coherent, Human, Accessible, Recoverable, Mindful, and Efficient Design. I find it a helpful guide through the design process, especially when working with a team.

Coherent

Everything feels like it belongs.

C1Adopt a design system and only use components from the system.

Use a set of standards throughout the product. Consistent look and feel evokes coherence.

C2The design system itself is CHARMED.

Consider this when adding or updating the system in Converge, or during periodic reviews of the system.

C3The visual representation of hierarchy and relationships is coherent across screens in a flow.

Screens in a flow share common elements. These elements should be represented in visually coherent ways.

Human

It speaks the customer’s language.

H1Language conforms to an adopted style guide.

Use a set of editorial best practices to ensure the product speaks with one voice. Language is useful and usable, embraces principles of clear writing, and adheres to brand attributes.

Accessible

It’s accessible to users of varying abilities on various devices.

A1Products follow all criteria of WCAG 2.1.

Following these guidelines will make content accessible to a wide range of people with disabilities, including accommodations for blindness and low vision, deafness and hearing loss, limited movement, speech disabilities, photosensitivity, and combinations of these, and some accommodation for learning disabilities and cognitive limitations.

A2The quality is equal on all supported devices, browsers, and viewports.

Adopt a standard set of devices, browsers, and viewports.

A3The responsibilities of a screen are not written as affordances.

“Offer a path to sign in” is a well-phrased responsibility. “Show a sign in link” conflates a responsibility with its affordance. Be mindful that laptops, phones, keyboards and assistive devices have different interaction models.

A4The hierarchy outline of a screen serves as a guide for HTML markup and keyboard navigation.

When developing a hierarchy outline, imagine tabbing through it. This should be how it feels to tab through the interface. This criterion supports A1.

Recoverable

Potential errors are prevented up front, and errors are easy to recover from.

R1All potential errors are understood.

This sounds obvious, but anytime the user is told something like “Whoops, an error occurred”, we obviously don't understand the error.

R2All potential errors are handled according to their type and preventability.

The type (e.g. invalid input) and preventability (e.g. guidable) of each potential error is understood. According to its type and preventability, handle it appropriately.

Mindful

The design thinks so the customer doesn’t have to.

M1There aren’t too many things on a screen.

Present 5-9 things on any one screen, using chunking and visual hierarchy to distinguish them. If necessary, divide the screen into steps. Otherwise the screen will burden the user’s short-term memory, and choosing the correct way to continue in the flow will become more time-consuming.

M2The things on a screen are self-evident, or at least self-explanatory.

A screen that doesn’t make the user think inspires thoughts like “OK, there's a ____, and that’s a ____, and there’s the thing that I want.”

M3The visual design of a screen is grounded in its hierarchy outline.

The hierarchy outline defines the order of elements and how they are logically related. Translating that to visual design: Things that are related in the hierarchy outline are related visually. Group them under a heading, display them in the same visual style, and/or put them in an area with a clearly defined boundary. Things are “nested” visually to show what’s part of what.

M4Use widely-adopted, conventional design patterns.

Users spend most of their time on other sites. Simplify their learning process by providing familiar design patterns.

M5Don’t float headings between paragraphs.

Headings should be closer to the text that follows them than the text that precedes them.

Efficient

It’s easy to perform a task or find information.

E1A flow has as few screens as it needs, but not too few.

“Fewer taps” always seems like the best approach. But there are valid reasons to use more screens: to simplify choices (E2) by breaking complex tasks into smaller steps, and to ensure (M2) there aren’t too many things on a screen. More clicks won’t hurt, as long as each click is a mindless, unambiguous choice.

E2Choices are easy to make.

The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices: which link to click, which text to read, which radio button to select, how to enter something into a text field. Limit the amount of decisions a user has to make to achieve their goal, and highlight recommended options.

E3The more important something is, the more prominent it is.

To ensure the user notices something, make it impossible to miss. Important things are larger, bolder, in a distinctive color, set off by more whitespace, nearer the top of the screen, or some combination of these.

E4Things intended to be easily selectable are large and easily within reach.

The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target. This especially applies to buttons, which should be easy to find and select.

E5What’s clickable is obvious. And if something looks clickable, it’s clickable.

As a user, I should never have to devote a millisecond to whether things are clickable or not.
