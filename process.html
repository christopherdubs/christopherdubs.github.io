Design process

Understand, Diverge, Converge, Deliver

Here's how this process has worked specifically for me, in product design, with a small team, diverse stakeholders, complex requirements, and tight timelines. But I find these four steps helpful in solving just about any problem.

1. Understand

Get everyone on the same page.

Establish a shared understanding of the problem, the desired outcome, the stakeholders, and the rough plan of action. Define the following:

  • Why we are doing this, how it fits into larger strategy
  • Desired business outcomes, e.g. moving metric X by Y%
  • Users and their goals
  • Stakeholders and their roles (e.g. accountable, responsible, consulted)
  • When we want to deliver a solution to customers

This step should not take more than one day. The point is to quickly agree to expectations and get to work.

2. Diverge

Dig in, consider possibilities, and structure potential solutions.

This is a meaty and very important step.

  • Do some background research. If there’s an existing solution, review how it’s performing, what customers think of it, and our opinions of it. Talk to subject matter experts and explore what competitors do.
  • Consider more than one approach to solving the problem. Sketch these out, and make notes about why or why not we might develop them further.
  • Model the selected approaches as screens and flows. For each screen, define its responsibilities, their relative importance, the heirarchy, and the potential errors.
  • Create lo-fi visual representations of these foundations: wireframes or prototypes. Don’t get into visual details yet; there’s a whole next step for that.

Review with stakeholders, and together, decide which approach(es) to refine. If unsure, bring multiple approaches forward to test.

3. Converge

Define exactly what we intend to deliver.

OK, now it’s OK to open Sketch.

  • Create high-fidelity comps or prototypes. Try different visual treatments, as long as they share the foundations established in Diverge. These should feel like natural evolutions, with no big surprises.
  • Write all final copy.
  • Demonstrate clearly how all contexts and potential errors are handled.
  • Prepare any new patterns or components for inclusion in the design system.

Review with stakeholders, and together, decide what to build. There may be two or more approaches to test.

4. Deliver

Build, test, and deliver it to customers.

Depending on who’s writing code, either do that or be available to help answer questions and clarify anything that was left ambiguous in the last step. Help ensure it works as intended.

Review with stakeholders, and make the decision to ship.

Acknowledgments

This process comes directly from my experience on the design team at LendUp. We worked with the GV design team from time to time, and inspired by their sprint process, we created a version of it for us to use on any project.
